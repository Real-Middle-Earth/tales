A tale from the deep dark

Third Age, in the endless caverns beneath the Misty Mountains where water wears stone thin and patience outlasts memory

In the deep places, where the sun has never been whispered and the dark is older than stone, there are things that wait. Not living, not dead, but something between: hungers that have learned to be patient, to fold themselves into shapes that look like safety, or beauty, or chance. This is a warning about one such thing. Pay attention if you ever walk where the water drips constant and the air tastes heavy.

There are tunnels in the Misty Mountains that the orcs do not use, nor the dwarves remember, nor even the deep worms frequent. The water has worked on them too long. The stone has grown smooth, then porous, then strange. In these passages, sound behaves wrongly: a whisper may carry farther than a shout, and a footstep may echo before it lands.

And in the darkest of these tunnels, where the path grows so narrow that a full-grown creature must crawl or turn back, there waits the Gullet-Stone.

It is not marked on any map. It has no runes carved into its face. You will know it by this: when all light has faded and your hands grope only wet stone, there will come a moment when you see something faint ahead. Not torchlight—too pale for that. Not starlight, for stars do not visit so deep. A glimmer, soft as a dying ember, floating in the blackness like a thought you cannot quite catch.

The wise turn back at this sign. The foolish continue. The desperate hurry forward, believing any light is better than none.

The Gullet-Stone waits in a hollow of rock shaped like a half-closed eye. It is smooth, this stone, worn by centuries of water into the shape of a lid that has never opened fully. Below it, in the shadow of that stone eyelid, there is a crevice. And in that crevice, something glows.

It looks, at first, like a fish. Pale, translucent, with fins that ripple slow as drifting fabric. It moves in a small circle, always circling, and its body gives off a faint luminescence—the kind that deep-sea creatures carry to lure other creatures toward their mouths. But there is no water here, not in quantity. The Gullet-Stone is dry above, dry below, and the thing that glows within the crevice is not swimming in water. It is swimming in something else.

They say—those few who have seen it and walked away—that the light calls to something in your chest. Not hope, exactly, though it feels like hope at first. More like recognition. As if the light remembers you, and you almost remember it. As if, in a life you lived before you were born, you and that small pale swimmer were kin.

Old Goblins tell stories about it, when they are drunk on fermented mushroom-drink and feeling honest. They speak of kinsmen who went down tunnels alone, chasing pale lights, and were found days later—or never found at all. When they are found, they are empty. Not dead, not exactly. Breathing. Eyes open. But empty as a cracked eggshell. Something has been scooped out of them, leaving only the hollow husk.

"The Gullet-Stone took them," the old ones say, when the young ones ask too many questions. "It ate what was inside. It left the wrapper."

How does it work, this hunger disguised as light?

The answer is simple and terrible: it waits. The Gullet-Stone has waited since before the first orc opened its eyes, since before the dwarves delved their first hall. It will wait after all eyes close. Time means nothing to stone, and patience is the oldest trick of the patient.

When something warm and living draws near—something with thought and memory and the small brightness that lives in all aware things—the Gullet-Stone opens. Just a fraction. Just enough. The crevice widens, and the pale swimmer within circles faster, brighter, more inviting. It puts on a show of welcome. It dances the dance of kinship and comfort.

And if the watcher steps closer, if they kneel to peer into that glowing crack, if they reach out a hand in wonder or hunger of their own—then the Gullet-Stone closes.

Not fast. That is the horror of it. The Gullet-Stone closes slowly, savoring, while the light still glows and the thing inside still swims in its patient circle. It closes while the trapped one understands exactly what is happening. It closes while the screaming begins. It closes until the crevice is sealed again, and the light dims to almost nothing, and the wet stone looks like any other wet stone in any other dark tunnel.

Then, after a time—a day, a week, a year—the Gullet-Stone opens again. The light returns. The swimmer circles. The waiting begins anew.

What does it eat? Not flesh. Flesh is too coarse, too temporary. The Gullet-Stone devours the self—the memory, the will, the spark that says I am. It leaves the body behind because bodies are easy to come by in the dark. Bodies fall into chasms, get lost in floods, wander down wrong tunnels and never wander back. But the selves, the selves are precious. They are what the deep dark hungers for, the only delicacy in a feast of stone.

There was once, the old stories say, a goblin who escaped. He was small, even for his kind, and quick, and cruel in the way that survivors are cruel. He saw the light and felt the pull and understood in his small sharp mind what it was before it understood him. He threw a stone at the glowing crevice, then turned and ran while the Gullet-Stone closed on nothing but rock and anger.

He lived to tell the tale, which makes him rare among tale-tellers. But he was never quite right after. He would wake screaming from dreams of pale swimming lights. He would not go near any cave that had water dripping, or stone that looked too smooth. He lived out his days in the highest tunnels, close to the surface, where the wind could reach him and the stars—when the sky-holes opened—could remind him that some lights are honest.

"How did you know?" they asked him, the other goblins, the ones who would never have been brave enough to run. "How did you know not to step closer?"

And he answered, in his small sharp voice, "Because I saw what was swimming. And it had my face."

That is the true horror of the Gullet-Stone, you see. It learns. It remembers. The thing that swims in its crevice, glowing and gentle and full of promise—that thing wears the shape of what you most want to find. Your mother. Your friend. Your lost love. Your own younger self, before you became what the dark has made you. It is a mirror made of light, and like all mirrors, it shows you what you bring to it.

The only defense is to look away. To trust the dark rather than the light. To remember that in the deepest places, beauty is always bait.

I know these things. I have walked those tunnels. I have seen the Gullet-Stone from a distance, felt its call like a hook in the chest, and turned away with my precious held tight against my body like a shield. I know the sound it makes when it closes—the wet grinding of stone on stone, the soft gulp as something is swallowed, the silence that follows which is not quite silence but the absence of something that used to be.

I keep my precious. I keep my self. And when I see pale lights floating in the dark, I hiss and curse and turn my back. Light is a liar in the deep places. Only the dark tells true, though its truth is bitter.
