The Whistle in the Hawthorn

a Shire-side remembrance of a tune caught on thorns

A small Shire tale

Fourth Age, the Hill-country of Hobbiton

There are things a hedge will keep for you if you are patient and polite: a button lost in haste, a ribbon torn in laughter, a penny bright as a new moon. Thorns have a way of saying not yet and now. They are not unkind, only careful. They do not like a hand that snatches.

I learned this, properly, one soft morning when the dew lay on the grass like a million small, cold promises and the larks were already practising their most serious pieces above The Hill. I was walking the lane — as one does, to put the head right — and I noticed a hawthorn hedge, newly white with blossom, had a curious glint in it, as if a star had come down for a rest and forgotten to go back.

It was not a star, of course. It was a whistle.

Small — no longer than my thumb — and made of silver that had not tarnished, though it must have lain there through more than one rain. It hung on a thread of green twine, snagged and held fast between two sharp twigs. The whistle looked very tidy, which is to say: completely out of place in a hedge, where everything is meant to be a little muddled, and nobody minds.

I did not take it at once. This is a point on which I pride myself (and on which I am frequently improved by my neighbours). I stood and considered, as though I were choosing a cake from a tray. The hedge was on the edge of a field belonging to the Cottons, and it ran alongside a lane that had seen more travellers in the last few years than it had seen in the previous few lifetimes. There had been a war, you see — and after the war, a kind of travelling that is not the same as running away, nor quite the same as seeking adventure. It is the sort that happens when folk have seen a horizon and cannot quite forget it.

I suppose I was thinking of all that when a voice said, behind me, “Oh! There it is.”

It was a lad I knew by sight: Tom Cotton’s youngest, brown-haired and quick, with an expression that suggested he had recently been instructed not to do something and had immediately begun doing it in his imagination. He had a basket on his arm and a smear of flour on his cheek, which is the Shire’s honest badge.

“Is it yours?” I asked, nodding at the hedge. (I did not, please note, call it my hedge. The Shire is full of arguments that begin with the words my hedge.)

He came close, but not too close — the way a sensible person approaches a nettle. “Not mine,” he said. “It was Mr. Greenhand’s.”

“Mr. Greenhand?”

“That’s what we called him,” the lad said, lowering his voice as though the hedge itself might be listening. “The one who came with the Bounders. Not a hobbit. Tall. Cloak. He smiled like he’d forgotten how, but was trying.”

That narrowed it down to a great many folk in those days, but I knew of whom he spoke. There had been a Man — a Ranger, as they are called — who helped set things right in the Shire, and then, when all was mended, did not linger to be thanked. Such folk are like the wind: you can feel what they have done long after they have passed.

“He gave it to you?” I asked.

The lad shook his head. “He gave it to my sister.” He turned red as a berry and pretended to examine a blossom with tremendous interest. “And she gave it back. She said it was too fine.”

I could not help smiling. “Was it too fine?”

“No,” he said, too quickly. Then, with a kind of stubborn fairness: “Yes.”

He told me, in fits and starts, what I might have guessed: that Mr. Greenhand had stopped at their gate one evening, weary enough to lean on the post, and asked for a cup of water. Mrs. Cotton gave him water and bread and a look that said, in the plain Shire tongue, If you are up to no good, you may sit down and be ashamed of it here where I can see you.

He sat down. He drank. He watched the children playing in the lane as though he had once been a child and had misplaced the memory. When he rose to go, he took out the little whistle and twirled it between his fingers — not carelessly, but as if he were thinking his thoughts through his hands.

“For luck,” he said, and held it out.

The sister — Rosie, I learned — took it because you cannot refuse a gift from someone who has clearly practised giving very little. She thanked him, and he nodded, and then he walked away down the road toward Bywater, and on toward wherever such folk go when they are not needed.

“And she gave it back?” I said.

“She tried,” the lad muttered. “She ran after him. But he’d gone.”

So Rosie did what hobbits do when they have something that does not fit in any drawer: she found a place for it that was safe and slightly inconvenient, and she told herself she would deal with it later. She tied it to a bit of twine and hung it in the hawthorn as a marker — right there by the bend, where the lane turns and you can see the Hill. “If he comes back,” she had said, “he’ll see it.”

“Did he?” I asked softly.

The lad stared at his shoes. “No.”

A breeze came then, just enough to shake the blossoms and send a few petals down like quiet, white questions. The whistle swung, caught a glimmer of light, and made no sound at all. I felt, suddenly, as old as the Party Tree, and almost as foolish.

“Do you know how to play it?” I asked.

He looked up, startled. “No. It’s for Men.”

“A whistle is for anyone with breath,” I said. “And a mind for listening.”

Now, I must confess: I did not know if it would make any sound. Silver can be stubborn. And there are whistles that have been made for signals in wild places, which means they speak in a language of distance and danger. But the thing in the hedge looked as innocent as a spoon, and I had a suspicion that it was meant to be heard on a windy hill, not in a tavern.

I reached into the hawthorn with care — not forcing, not jerking — and the hedge, after pricking me once for good measure, released the twine. The whistle was cool in my palm, as if it had been waiting.

“Go on,” the lad said, and his voice cracked on the words. “Try.”

I put it to my lips, and I blew.

It was not loud. It was not the sort of sound that makes dogs bark and Bounders sit up straight. It was a clear, thin note that seemed to travel along the lane itself, like a finger tracing a familiar line on a map. It rose, held, and then — and here is the odd part — it turned. Not in pitch, but in feeling, as if the note had found a door and stepped through it into another room.

The larks above the Hill fell silent. Not for long — only for a breath or two — but long enough that I knew it had not been my imagination. Somewhere, far off, a thrush answered: a single, bright call, like a penny dropped into a well.

The lad’s eyes went wide. “That’s…” he began, and could not finish.

Because down the lane, beyond the bend, where the hedge and the grass hide the road for a time, a figure had stopped.

Only a figure: a Man, tall even at that distance, with a cloak the colour of old leaves. He stood very still, as if he had heard his own name spoken by someone he had not expected to meet again. Then he lifted a hand — not a wave, not quite — and turned his head toward the Hill, toward the round doors and chimneys, toward the place that had once been guarded and then, for a time, lost, and then won back again by folk who had no business being brave but were, all the same.

He did not come closer. Perhaps he had promised himself he would not. Perhaps he thought it kinder to leave the Shire untouched by his footsteps, now that it was healing. Perhaps he was simply passing by, and the whistle had caught him like a hook catches a sleeve.

But he stood there long enough for the lad to swallow and whisper, “Mr. Greenhand.”

Then the figure nodded — once — and in that nod there was a great deal: thanks, farewell, and something like relief. He turned, and he was gone beyond the rise, as quietly as he had come.

The lane was empty again. The larks resumed their work with a kind of embarrassed haste, as if they had been caught eavesdropping. The lad let out the breath he had been holding and looked at me as though I had conjured a dragon out of a teacup.

“What do we do now?” he asked.

I closed the whistle in my hand. It felt warmer already, as if it were pleased with itself.

“Now,” I said, “you tell your sister that her hedge has done its duty.”

He frowned. “And the whistle?”

I looked at the hawthorn, still white with blossom, still sharp, still keeping its little secrets. “We put it somewhere it belongs,” I said. “Where it can be found by someone who is meant to hear it — and where it will not be forgotten in a drawer.”

So we went up the lane together, and I hung the whistle inside the old post-box by the Party Field — the one that nobody uses now, because hobbits prefer to knock, and because nearly everyone can be reached by shouting over a hedge. I left it there on its twine, and on the lid I wrote, in as small a hand as I could manage:

For luck, and for roads that lead home.

And if you are wondering whether the whistle remains there still, I cannot say. Post-boxes have their own politics. But sometimes, when the wind is right and the morning is kind, I fancy I hear a thin, clear note in the distance — and the larks, for one heartbeat, listen too.
