The Crumb in the Crack

a short tale of small hungers in dark places

A short tale

Third Age, in the endless dark beneath the Misty Mountains

In the deep places, where no sun has ever ventured and the only light is the faint glow of things that should not shine, a story can begin with something as small as a crumb. This is the tale of such a crumb—how it came to be there, and what found it, and what happened after. It is not a grand tale. The great stories speak of rings and kings and battles that shake the mountains. This one speaks only of hunger, and patience, and the strange mercy that even darkness sometimes shows to small things.

The crumb fell from the pocket of a dwarven traveler who was running for his life. This was in the year 2841 by the Shire Reckoning, though no Shire-folk would hear of these events for many years, and perhaps that was for the best. The dwarf's name was Óin, son of Gróin, though names matter little where the crumb landed.

He was fleeing through a narrow passage, one of countless such cracks that split the mountains like old scars. Behind him came sounds that no dwarf wishes to hear: the padding of bare feet, the click of stone on stone, the wet breathing of things that live entire lives in absolute dark. He clutched his axe in one hand and his last lump of waybread in the other. The waybread was old, hard as saddle-leather, precious beyond gold.

In his panic, as he squeezed through a gap barely wide enough for his shoulders, a piece no larger than his thumbnail broke free. It fell, tumbling end over end, and landed in a crack where two stones met at an angle. There it wedged, stuck by its own roughness and by the damp that made the stone cling.

Óin did not notice. He ran on, and by luck or fate or the mercy of the Valar, he found his way to the eastern slopes and eventually to the Lonely Mountain, where he lived to tell his tale to any who would buy him a drink. But he never knew about the crumb.

For thirteen years it lay there. In the counting of the deep places, this was nothing—a breath, a heartbeat. But for a crumb, even one made by elvish hands, thirteen years is a long time to wait.

It did not rot. Elvish waybread does not rot, exactly, any more than stone rots. But it changed. The damp worked into its edges, softening them. Minerals from the dripping water crusted its surface, so that it looked less like bread and more like a strange fungus, pale and slightly luminescent. And it began, very faintly, to smell.

The smell was not unpleasant, not exactly. It smelled of sunlight on wheat fields, of clean air, of something growing rather than decaying. In the dark beneath the mountains, this was a smell from another world, a world that had forgotten the tunnels existed.

Things came to investigate.

First came the pale beetles, eyeless and patient, feeling their way along the crack with antennae like threads of silver. They tasted the crumb and found it strange—too rich, too complicated, too full of light. They left it alone and went back to eating the slow growths that filmed the wet stone.

Then came a rat, or something like a rat, though it had never seen daylight. It sniffed the crumb for a long time, whiskers trembling. Hunger urged it to bite, but caution held it back. In the dark, strange things can be poison. The rat withdrew, backward, never taking its eyes from the crumb until it was far enough away to turn and scurry.

Weeks passed. The smell spread, faint but distinct, through the branching tunnels. It reached a pool where something lived that had once been a fish, or perhaps had never been a fish at all. The thing in the pool stirred, sensing the promise of something that came from above. But it could not leave its water, and the crumb was dry in its crack, and so the thing subsided back into its dreams of currents.

And then, on a night that was no different from any other night (for all nights are the same in the deep), something small and thin and desperate found the crack.

It was not a goblin. Goblins hunt in packs, make noise, carry light. This thing moved silently, close to the stone, pausing often to listen. It was small enough to squeeze through gaps where goblins would stick. It was patient enough to search for food where others would have given up long before. It was hungry—hungrier than the beetles, hungrier than the rat, hungrier than the thing in the pool. It had been hungry for so long that hunger had become its nature, as much as the dark had become its home.

It found the crumb by smell, following the impossible scent of sunlight through tunnels where sunlight had never been. When it reached the crack, it hesitated. Traps were common in the deep. Many things that seemed like food were not. The goblins sometimes set baits, or worse, simply waited in the dark for smaller things to wander past.

But there was no sound of breathing but its own. No smell of goblin, which was unmistakable—sweat and iron and old meat. Only the smell of wheat and sunlight and something that made its mouth ache with memory.

It reached out one long finger and touched the crumb.

The crumb was soft now, yielding. It broke apart at the touch, smaller than before, almost nothing. The thing lifted it carefully, as if it might crumble to dust. It brought it close to its face, though there was no light to see by, and smelled it again—really smelled it this time, drawing the scent deep into lungs that had forgotten what good air tasted like.

It ate.

The taste was overwhelming. It tasted of everything the thing had lost—green things growing, water that ran in open air, warmth on skin, voices speaking kindly. It tasted of a life that had happened so long ago that it might have been a dream, or might have been real, or might have been both.

The thing sat in the dark, in the crack where the two stones met, and felt the crumb dissolve on its tongue. It was too small to fill its stomach. It did not satisfy hunger. It only made the hunger sharper, more specific, more aware of itself.

But for a moment—for exactly as long as the taste lasted—the thing remembered that it had once had a name.

It could not recall the name. The name was gone, dissolved like the crumb, leaving only the shape of its absence. But the memory that there had been a name—that remained. That could not be eaten. That could not be taken away.

The thing sat for a long time in the crack. Eventually it moved on, back into the tunnels, back to the search for food that would keep it alive, which is different from food that reminds you what alive means. But it was changed, subtly, the way a stone is changed when water drips on it for years. Something had been added. Something small, but real.

The crumb was gone. The crack was empty. The smell of sunlight faded, absorbed into the damp stone, remembered only by the beetle who had tasted it and rejected it, and even the beetle could not have said why it sometimes paused in its endless patrols, why it sometimes turned toward the upper tunnels as if waiting for something that never came.

Years later, when a different traveler passed this way—a hobbit, small and frightened, with a companion who glowed faintly in the dark—neither of them noticed the crack. Why would they? It was only a crack. The mountain was full of cracks. They hurried past, and the crack held its secret, and the dark held the crack.

But some say—those who listen to beetles, and to rats, and to the things that live in pools—that if you put your ear to the stone in that particular passage, you might still hear it. Not the crumb, which is long gone. But the echo of the taste. The memory of wheat growing in sunlight. The whisper of a name that was once spoken in kindness.

It is a small sound. Most would miss it. But to those who have been hungry long enough, hunger teaches a kind of listening.

And in the deep places, beneath the roots of mountains, listening is sometimes all that remains.
