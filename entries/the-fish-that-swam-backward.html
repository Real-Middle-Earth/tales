A riddle-tale

Third Age, in the deep places where the sun is forgotten and the water remembers

There are fish in the dark waters below the mountains that have never seen the sun nor the moon nor the stars. They live by other senses, in a world of cold currents and ancient silence. This is the tale of one such fish—not the largest, nor the fiercest, but the only one that ever asked questions. Hunters who trap the streams of the Misty Mountains speak of it still, when the fire burns low and they wish to frighten the young.

In the deeps beneath the roots of the Misty Mountains, where the water runs black and cold as the heart of a stone, there lived a fish without eyes. It had no need of them. The dark was its mother, and it knew the tunnels by the taste of the current and the press of water against its fins.

It was an old fish, though no one could have said how old. Time moves differently in the deep, measured not by days but by the slow shifting of rock and the occasional fall of stone from above. The fish had seen none of these things, for it was blind, but it had felt them through the water, as you might feel thunder through your feet before the sound arrives.

For most of its life, the fish behaved as fish do. It ate what drifted down from above—bits of matter that had once been alive, now softened by the long descent through fissures and cracks. It hid when the water grew warm with some distant shaking of the earth. It spawned once, or perhaps twice, though in the dark it was hard to tell one season from another.

But one day—or one night, for the difference meant nothing there—the fish did something that no fish had done before.

It began to swim backward.

Not in alarm, as fish sometimes do when startled. Not in play, for fish in the sunless deep do not play. It swam backward slowly, deliberately, against the current that had carried it since it was a speck in the egg. It swam as if it were searching for something it had lost, or something it had never had but suddenly desired.

The other fish noticed, as much as fish notice anything. They gave it room, for in the dark there are dangers, and anything strange might be dangerous. But the backward-swimming fish paid them no mind. It went on, fin by fin, against the pull of the water, seeking the source.

Now, in the deepest places there are currents that run in directions no surface-dweller could understand. They branch and split and turn back on themselves, guided by pressures and temperatures invisible to eyes. The fish followed the main current upward—yes, upward, though it had never known up from down—until it came to a place where the water grew strange.

Here the current was warmer, thick with minerals that tasted like metal and memory. Here the walls of the tunnel were smooth as if rubbed by countless years of passing. And here, the fish felt, something was waiting.

It was not a thing that could be eaten. It was not a predator, though there were predators in the deep—blind things with rows of needle teeth that glowed faintly in the absolute dark. No, this was something else. The fish felt it as a kind of pressure on its mind, or what passed for a mind in a creature with no need of thought.

Why do you swim backward? the presence asked. Not in words, for fish do not know words. But in a question nonetheless, pressed into the water like a shape pressed into mud.

The fish had no answer. It had never answered anything before. It only knew that it was tired of going forward, tired of the endless drift, tired of eating and hiding and spawning and dying without ever knowing why. It wanted to see where it had come from. It wanted to understand.

You are small, said the presence. You are blind. You are nothing in the great dark. What do you hope to find?

And because the fish had no voice, it answered in the only way it could: it swam on. Upward, against the current, toward the source of the warmth.

The presence did not stop it. Perhaps it was amused. Perhaps it was curious itself, in its ancient, patient way. In the deep, curiosity is a rare thing, and even the ancient powers value rarity.

The fish swam for what might have been days or weeks or years. In the dark, time is a story told by those who have light to see clocks. The fish told no stories. It only swam.

And at last it came to a pool.

Not a large pool, as surface-dwellers measure such things. But large enough that the fish could not feel the far walls, no matter how it turned. Large enough that the water here was still, undisturbed by any current. Large enough that something had lived here for a very long time, alone in the dark, gathering secrets like other fish gather food.

At the center of the pool was an island. Not of rock, but of things that had fallen from above and settled over ages—bones and tools and rings and coins and the small, hard possessions of creatures who had once walked in the light. The island shifted and breathed with the movements of what lived upon it, and what lived there was old beyond counting.

The fish approached. It did not know fear, for fish that live in total darkness know nothing to compare with the dark. But it felt something like wonder. It had never felt the bottom of a pool before. It had never known stillness.

And on the island, something stirred.

You are the one who swims backward, said the thing. The small, blind seeker. I have felt you coming for a long time.

The fish waited. It had no choice. The stillness of the pool held it like a hand.

You want to know the answer, said the thing. The answer to what you have never asked. You want to know why. Why the current flows. Why the dark is dark. Why you are small and blind and brief, while the mountains endure.

The fish felt the truth of this. It had not known, until that moment, that questions existed. But now it knew, and it wanted the answer with a want stronger than hunger, stronger than the drive to spawn, stronger than life itself.

I will tell you, said the thing. But first, a bargain. You have come seeking wisdom. Wisdom must be paid for. Give me something in return.

The fish had nothing. It was flesh and bone and the stubborn will to swim against the stream. It had no gold, no secrets, no name to trade.

Give me your fins, said the thing. Give me your scales. Give me your flesh, when you are done with it. Give me your swim-bladder and your bones. Give me all that you are, piece by piece, and I will tell you the answer.

And the fish, being only a fish, and hungry for knowing, agreed.

So the thing on the island began to speak, and as it spoke, the fish felt itself growing smaller. Its fins shrunk. Its scales loosened and fell away, sinking to join the island's substance. Its flesh grew thin. But it listened, oh yes, it listened with every particle of its being, for the answer was coming, the great answer to everything.

The current flows, said the thing, because it must. The dark is dark because light is precious. You are small because the world is large. You are blind because sight is a gift given only to those who face the sun. You are brief because permanence is for stone alone. And the reason—the great reason, the answer to your unasked question—is this: there is no reason. There is only the current, and the dark, and the swimming. That is all. That has always been all.

The fish felt itself almost gone. It was a shadow of a shadow, a thought of bone and memory. But it had the answer now. It had paid for it, and it was the answer.

And in its last moment, the fish did a strange thing. It laughed.

Not a laugh as men laugh, or even as fish might laugh if fish could laugh. But a kind of release into the water, a bubble of final understanding. For if the answer was that there was no answer, then the question had been wrong. And if the question was wrong, then swimming backward was as good as swimming forward, and the seeking was the only truth, and the dark was not empty after all—it was full of the possibility of light.

The fish dissolved into the pool, scales and bones and the last flicker of its backward will. The thing on the island gathered up its payment and was satisfied, for it had spoken truly, and truth is a kind of nourishment in the deep.

But something remained.

A current, where before there had been stillness. A small current, barely perceptible, flowing not downward toward the distant sunless seas, but upward, toward places where the water grew warmer and the rock grew thinner. A current that said, without words: Keep swimming. Keep asking. The answer is the asking.

And in the ages since, other fish have felt it. Small fish, curious fish, fish dissatisfied with the drift. They turn, sometimes, against the pull of the deep, and they swim upward toward a light they cannot imagine and do not need to believe in. They do not reach it. But they swim.

And in the villages above the mountains, by the streams that carry water from the high places to the valleys below, there are old folk who know this tale. They catch fish in wicker traps, and sometimes they find one that fights the current, that will not be taken, that seems to look at them with eyes that see something they cannot.

These fish they throw back. Not out of superstition, or not only. But out of recognition. For they know, as the fisher-folk of every age have known, that some things are more valuable than a meal. Some things must be allowed to continue their journey, even if the journey makes no sense.

Especially then.

Especially when the dark is deep and the answers are empty and the only truth is the stubborn, backward, impossible swimming against the stream.
